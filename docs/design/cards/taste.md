# Taste Cards

## Role in the Game
Taste cards represent Uganda's traditional foods, drinks, and culinary traditions. Played during a Journey to match the active Destination's region. The player reads the food fact aloud before playing — a moment of laughter, curiosity, and shared discovery around the table.

## Count
**24 cards** in the deck. Each player starts with 2.

## Front Design

### Layout
- **Top left** — Region badge: colored circle with bold letter (C / W / N / E)
- **Top right** — Small label: "Taste" in uppercase tracking
- **Center** — Full bleed painterly illustration of the food/drink being prepared or served
- **Bottom** — Dark gradient overlay with food name (bold serif) and region below

### Illustration Direction
Food as art. Close-up, appetising, cultural context. Show the food being cooked over an open fire, served on banana leaves, or held by hands. Steam, texture, warmth. Not a sterile food photo — a vivid, hand-painted illustration that makes you hungry and curious.

## Cards in the Deck

### Central Region (C — Green)

| Card | Fact | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Rolex | The Rolex was invented in Kampala — an omelette rolled inside a chapati. The name comes from "rolled eggs." | Uganda's most iconic street food, sold at every roadside kiosk |
| Matooke | Matooke (steamed green banana) is Uganda's national dish — eaten daily by over 70% of Ugandans. | Steamed in banana leaves, served with groundnut sauce or meat stew |
| Groundnut Stew | Groundnut stew (ebinyebwa) is Uganda's most beloved sauce — slow-cooked peanuts with vegetables and spices. | Served with matooke, rice, or posho across all regions |
| Roasted Goat (Nyama Choma) | "Nyama Choma" means "roasted meat" in Swahili — in Uganda it is almost always goat, cooked over open charcoal. | A social food — always eaten in groups, always with company |
| Mandazi | Mandazi are East African fried doughnuts flavoured with coconut milk and cardamom — eaten at breakfast. | Crispy outside, soft inside — sold in markets every morning |
| Nsenene (Grasshoppers) | Nsenene (long-horned grasshoppers) are a Ugandan delicacy — harvested twice a year during rainy season. | Fried with onion and salt — crunchy, protein-rich, and surprisingly delicious |

### Western Region (W — Blue)

| Card | Fact | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Eshabwe | Eshabwe is a Banyankole royal sauce made from ghee and salted butter — traditionally served to kings and honoured guests. | Rich, creamy, eaten with matooke or millet bread |
| Obushera | Obushera is a fermented millet or sorghum porridge drink from western Uganda — slightly sour and deeply nutritious. | Served cold in gourds — a traditional refreshment after harvest |
| Akaro | Akaro is a thick millet porridge from the Banyankole — eaten for breakfast and as a post-harvest celebration meal. | Dense, filling, and eaten with dried fish or sour milk |
| Ghee (Amavuta) | Western Uganda's Ankole cattle produce milk so rich in fat that their ghee (Amavuta) is considered the purest in East Africa. | Used in cooking, as a condiment, and as a traditional beauty product |
| Roasted Maize | Roasted maize on the cob is sold at every roadside stand in western Uganda — smoky, sweet, and eaten walking. | Rubbed with salt and chilli — a universally loved snack |
| Millet Bread (Karo) | Millet bread is the staple of western and northern Uganda — denser and more nutritious than wheat bread. | Made from finger millet, served with stews and soups |

### Northern Region (N — Yellow)

| Card | Fact | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Atapa | Atapa is the Acholi name for thick millet or sorghum bread — the staple food of northern Uganda, eaten at every meal. | Dense, filling, served with dried fish, meat stew, or sesame paste |
| Lalur (Sesame Paste) | Lalur is a sesame paste sauce from northern Uganda — nutritious, nutty, and eaten with millet bread or matooke. | Ground sesame seeds cooked with greens — a northern specialty |
| Malakwang | Malakwang is a traditional Acholi dish of boiled vegetable greens (often pumpkin leaves) cooked in sesame paste. | Bitter, nutritious, and deeply cultural — made by grandmothers across the north |
| Dried Nile Perch | Nile perch from the Albert Nile is dried in the sun and traded across Uganda — a protein staple in northern communities. | Smoked or sun-dried, it travels far and lasts long — essential for northern cooking |
| Simsim Candy | Simsim (sesame) candy is a northern Ugandan sweet — toasted sesame seeds bound with sugar or honey. | Crunchy, nutty, and sold at northern markets — a beloved after-school snack |
| Odii (Shea Butter Stew) | Odii is a northern Ugandan stew made with shea butter — the Shea tree (Omubu) grows abundantly in northern Uganda. | Rich, earthy, and fragrant — a traditional stew unique to the north |

### Eastern Region (E — Red)

| Card | Fact | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Malewa | Malewa is dried bamboo shoots from Mount Elgon — a delicacy of the Bagisu people, eaten as a snack or in stews. | Smoky, chewy, and unique — only found in eastern Uganda's highland areas |
| Luwombo | Luwombo is a royal Ugandan dish — meat, chicken, or mushrooms slow-steamed inside banana leaves for hours. | Originally cooked for Buganda King Mwanga II — now enjoyed across the country |
| Boo (Boiled Greens) | Boo refers to any wild leafy greens gathered and boiled in eastern Uganda — nutritious and deeply traditional. | Amaranth, cassava leaves, or pumpkin leaves — gathered fresh and cooked simply |
| Posho (Ugali) | Posho is maize flour cooked into a thick, stiff porridge — the most widely eaten staple across eastern Uganda. | Eaten with beans, groundnut stew, or meat — the everyday foundation of eastern meals |
| Roasted Groundnuts | Eastern Uganda grows much of the country's groundnuts — roasted and salted, they are eaten as a snack everywhere. | Sold in small paper cones at every market and roadside stand |
| Kwon Kal (Millet Balls) | Kwon Kal are millet flour balls from eastern Uganda — rolled by hand and eaten with soup or stew as a complete meal. | Sticky, dense, and nutritious — the original fast food of the east |
